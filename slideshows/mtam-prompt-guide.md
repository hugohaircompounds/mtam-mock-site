# How to Build Your Salon Website with AI — A Prompt Guide

You don't need to know code. You don't need a web designer. You just need to know what you want your site to feel like — and a conversation with AI can get you there.

This guide walks you through prompting Claude (or any AI assistant) to design a professional website for your hair salon. Each prompt is written so you can copy it, adjust the details for your business, and get back a section of your site that actually sounds like you. Think of it less like giving instructions to a computer and more like brainstorming with a really fast creative partner.

This guide was created alongside a real demo — a full homepage built entirely through AI prompting and intentional design thinking. Every prompt here comes from that process. The result was a page that didn't just look polished; every section existed for a reason. You can get the same results for your salon.

---

## Start with Your Story

Before your salon shows anyone what you offer, show them you understand where they are right now. The most powerful thing your salon website can do in the first thirty seconds is make a visitor think, "they get it."

> Tell Claude: "Write me a section that names the top 5 frustrations hair stylists face when trying to learn new techniques or find a great salon. Use second-person language — speak directly to the reader. Don't mention my salon name yet. Just validate their experience."

This works because your clients trust businesses that can name the problem before pitching the solution. Your visitor should feel seen before they feel sold to.

> Tell Claude: "Now write a short manifesto — two or three paragraphs — that explains my salon's philosophy. The tone should be confident but warm, like you're letting someone in on a secret. Talk about why we do things differently, not what we do. Avoid listing services. Focus on what we believe about our craft."

> Tell Claude: "Rewrite that manifesto so it directly addresses the frustrations from the previous section. If the frustrations were about feeling judged or pressured, the manifesto should speak to acceptance and encouragement. Connect the two sections emotionally."

> Tell Claude: "Write a values statement for my salon that takes a clear stance. Instead of saying 'we welcome everyone,' name the specific industry problem we reject — like gatekeeping, overpriced certifications, or rigid brand loyalty. Make it feel like a line in the sand, not a corporate mission statement."

---

## Make Your First Impression Count

Your hero section is the first thing anyone sees. It needs to say what the visitor gets — not what you offer. There's a difference. "We offer 50 services" is about you. "Walk out feeling like yourself again" is about them.

> Tell Claude: "Write a hero headline for my salon website. Instead of saying what we offer, say what the client achieves. Bad example: 'We offer 50+ extension techniques.' Good example: 'Master the techniques that make clients rebook.' Keep it under 12 words."

> Tell Claude: "Now write a subheadline — one or two sentences — that sits under the hero headline. It should address how the visitor wants to feel, not what services are available. Think about the emotional state someone is in when they land on your site for the first time."

> Tell Claude: "Write a call-to-action button label for the hero section. Use action language that describes what the visitor does, not what we do. 'Start Learning' is better than 'Subscribe Now.' 'Book Your Transformation' is better than 'See Our Services.' Give me 5 options and tell me why each one works."

> Tell Claude: "Write a short supporting paragraph for the hero section — two to three sentences max. It should explain what my salon does in plain language, mention the people behind it by first name, and end with a phrase that gives the reader permission to make their own choices."

---

## Show What You Teach

Whether your salon offers courses, services, or treatments, this section is where visitors figure out if you have what they need. The key is to describe your services by what the client walks away with — not what the service includes.

> Tell Claude: "Create 6 service cards for my salon. Each card should have a short title (2-4 words), and one sentence about what the client walks away with — not what the service includes. For example, instead of '60-minute deep conditioning treatment,' write 'Hair that feels like it did before the damage.' Suggest an icon keyword for each card."

> Tell Claude: "Look at the 6 service cards you just wrote. Do any of them describe features instead of outcomes? Rewrite any that do. Every card should answer the question: 'What changes for the client after this?'"

> Tell Claude: "Write a headline for the services section that makes visitors feel like they're looking at a menu of possibilities, not a list of products. Use confident, outcome-focused language. Give me 3 options."

> Tell Claude: "Add a one-sentence subtitle under the services headline that reassures the visitor they're not locked into one choice. Something that communicates flexibility and variety without sounding like a sales pitch."

---

## Introduce Your Team

People don't just hire salons — they hire people. Your team section should make visitors feel like they already know who they'll be working with. Introduce your team through personality and specialization, not credentials and years of experience.

> Tell Claude: "Create team member cards for my salon. Each card needs: a name, a specialization label (like 'Texture Specialist' or 'Color Correction Expert' — not 'Level 3 Certified'), and one sentence about their approach. The sentence should sound like something they'd actually say at a consultation, not a LinkedIn bio."

> Tell Claude: "Write a founders section for my salon. Tell the origin story through struggle, not credentials. Instead of 'With 20 years of experience,' start with the moment the founders realized the industry needed something different. Two to three short paragraphs. Conversational tone — like they're explaining it over coffee."

> Tell Claude: "Rewrite the founders section so the first paragraph names a specific frustration the founders personally experienced. The second paragraph explains the turning point — the moment they decided to build something new. The third paragraph connects their story back to the visitor: 'That's why we built this for you.'"

---

## Build Trust with Real Results

Testimonials are where visitors decide if people like them got results from your services. The key is specificity. "Great salon!" means nothing. "I stopped dreading color correction appointments" means everything. Your clients have stories — this section lets those stories do the selling.

> Tell Claude: "Write 3 testimonials for my salon. Each one should describe a specific thing the client changed in their routine or confidence after visiting — not just say we're great. Include a first name and city for each one. The tone should sound like a real person texting a friend about their experience."

> Tell Claude: "Review those testimonials. Do any of them use generic praise like 'amazing experience' or 'highly recommend'? Replace that language with specific outcomes — what changed in their daily routine, their confidence, or their client relationships."

> Tell Claude: "Write a section heading for the testimonials that focuses on change, not satisfaction. Instead of 'What Our Clients Say,' try something like 'What changed for our clients' or 'The results speak for themselves.' Give me 3 options."

---

## Price with Confidence

Your salon's pricing section shouldn't just list numbers — it should help visitors understand the value of your services compared to their alternatives. Anchor against what they'd spend elsewhere, and make the best option obvious.

> Tell Claude: "Create a 3-tier pricing layout for my salon's services or membership. Each tier should have: a name that describes commitment level (not 'Basic/Pro/Enterprise'), a price, a short list of what's included (4-5 bullet points), and a call-to-action button. Make the middle tier the best value and label it 'Most Popular.'"

> Tell Claude: "Add a comparison line below the pricing cards that anchors the value. Something like: 'A single certification costs $500-$2,500. Your membership gives you everything for less than $X per month.' Use the actual numbers from my pricing to calculate the comparison."

> Tell Claude: "Review the pricing tier names. Do they sound corporate or approachable? Rename them if needed. Good names feel like stages of a journey — 'Explore,' 'Commit,' 'Master' — not product SKUs."

> Tell Claude: "Write a one-sentence subtitle for the pricing section that addresses the most common objection — usually price or commitment. Frame it as permission: 'Full access, cancel anytime, no brand loyalty required.'"

---

## Close the Deal

Your final call-to-action is the last thing visitors see before they decide to become your clients. Use job-completion language — describe the action the visitor takes, not your salon's action. "Start Learning" is about them. "Subscribe Now" is about you.

> Tell Claude: "Write a final call-to-action section for my salon website. The headline should create gentle urgency without being pushy — focus on what the visitor's next client or next appointment could look like if they start now. Keep it under 15 words."

> Tell Claude: "Write a one-line subtitle under the final CTA headline. It should remove the last barrier to action. Something that says 'this is low-risk' without using the word 'risk.' Examples: 'Start learning today. Cancel anytime.' or 'Your first week is on us.'"

> Tell Claude: "Write a CTA button label for the bottom of the page. It should use the same language as the hero button for consistency. If the hero said 'Start Learning,' the footer CTA should say 'Start Learning' too — not 'Sign Up' or 'Join Now.'"

> Tell Claude: "Design a minimal footer for my salon website. Include only: copyright, a Terms link, and a Contact link. No social media icons, no secondary navigation, no newsletter signup. The page should feel complete, not cluttered."

## Tips for Getting the Best Results

AI works best as a conversation, not a single prompt. If the first result doesn't feel right, don't start over — tell Claude what to change.

Here are some follow-up prompts that work for any section:

> Tell Claude: "That's close, but the tone is too formal. Rewrite it like you're talking to a friend who just asked what my salon does."

> Tell Claude: "I like the structure but the language is too generic. Add specific details about hair extensions and color correction."

> Tell Claude: "Make it shorter. Cut any sentence that doesn't directly help the visitor make a decision."

> Tell Claude: "This reads like a corporate website. I want it to feel like a real person wrote it. Remove any sentence that starts with 'We are' or 'Our team.'"

The more you tell Claude about what you want to change, the better the results get. Think of it as a creative conversation — you're the expert on your business, and AI is the expert on putting your ideas into words that work on a website.
